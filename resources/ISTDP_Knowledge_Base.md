## 1. Triangle of Conflict — Detailed Model

### The Three Corners

Every patient statement is an expression of **one of three things**. The therapist's first task after every response is to determine which.

**FEELING**
- Not a cognitive event — a bio-physiological reality with three components:
  1. Cognitive label ("I am angry")
  2. Physiological arousal (sensation of heat, heaviness, tightening)
  3. Motoric impulse (arms rising, urge to cry, urge to reach out)
- Without all three: the patient is describing feelings rather than experiencing them
- Primary feelings: **anger, sadness, fear, joy, surprise**
- Composite feelings include love, guilt, grief — these carry their own somatic signatures

**ANXIETY**
- Not a thought ("I'm afraid of loss") — a bio-physiological activation in the body
- Three discharge pathways (see Section 2 below)
- Sigh = striated muscle discharge = unconscious feeling has been mobilised
- The sigh is the single most reliable signal that your intervention is working

**DEFENSE**
- Strategies to keep feelings and feeling-triggering thoughts out of awareness
- Four categories (see Section 3 below)
- Can be repressive (keep feeling down) or tactical (keep therapist at a distance)
- Character defenses = doing to self what was done by early attachment figures

### What to Do With Each

| Response Type | Intervention |
|---|---|
| Feeling | Encourage — "Stay there. Tell me more about that." |
| Anxiety (striated) | Note it, redirect to feeling — "You're anxious — what's the feeling under that?" |
| Anxiety (smooth/CPD) | **Regulate first** — slow down, breathe, ground before any feeling work |
| Defense | Name it + show price + invite return to feeling (one-two punch) |

## 2. Anxiety Pathways — The Critical Three

### Pathway 1: Striated (Voluntary) Muscle
**What you see:** muscle tension, sighing, hand clenching, leg movement, jaw tension, shoulder shrugging
**What it means:** patient can tolerate emotion — unconscious feeling is rising — proceed
**Associated defense:** isolation of affect (intellectual awareness without emotional experience)
**Clinical decision:** direct mobilisation of unconscious — apply pressure

### Pathway 2: Smooth (Involuntary) Muscle
**What you see:** nausea, GI distress, migraines, hypertension, diarrhoea — patient appears superficially calm but complains of physical symptoms
**What it means:** emotion is being channelled directly into the body before reaching consciousness (instant repression)
**Clinical decision:** **stop pressure** — regulate anxiety first — build capacity to tolerate — then return to feeling work

### Pathway 3: Cognitive-Perceptual Disruption (CPD)
**What you see:** blurry vision, mental confusion, ringing in ears, dissociation, losing train of thought, feeling disconnected or dreamlike
**What it means:** anxiety is overwhelming the cognitive system — projection and projective identification are primary defenses
**Clinical decision:** **stop immediately** — ground the patient — do not pursue feeling — work on capacity building

### Pathway 4: Motor Conversion (Abbass)
**What you see:** focal or global muscle weakness — body going limp or heavy
**What it means:** repression channelled into musculature — direct emotion suppression
**Clinical decision:** treat like smooth muscle pathway — regulate first

### Assessment Protocol
Always check **all pathways** — patients can have mixed pathways simultaneously.
Check in order: neck → chest → belly → vision → hearing → cognition clarity

## 3. Defence Taxonomy — Full ISTDP Classification

### Repressive Defences
Repress feelings so the patient is not aware of them.
- **Intellectualisation:** "I think he was rude" instead of feeling toward him
- **Rationalisation:** explaining why the feeling doesn't matter
- **Minimisation:** "not real happy" / "not particularly bothered"
- **Vagueness:** "it's more of a general thing" — blocks specific access
- **Displacement:** feeling toward X reported as feeling about Y
- **Reaction formation:** presenting the opposite of the actual feeling

### Regressive Defences
Involve regression in capacity to see reality.
- **Splitting:** extreme all-or-nothing framing
- **Projection:** "you're going to ask me things I don't want to talk about" (own desire projected)
- **Projective identification:** inducing therapist to act out projected role
- **Somatisation:** emotion converted directly to body symptom
- **Externalisation:** locating problem entirely in others/environment
- **Acting out:** discharging feeling through behaviour instead of experiencing it
- **Discharge:** emotional release without genuine experience

### Character Defences
Based on identifications with punitive early figures. Patient does to self what was done to them.
- Self-attack: criticising self the way they were criticised
- Self-dismissal: dismissing own feelings the way they were dismissed
- Self-neglect: treating self as not mattering, as they were treated
- **Key clinical marker:** the patient is reenacting an early relational dynamic on themselves

### Tactical Defences
Interpersonal — designed to keep the therapist at a distance.
- **Vagueness** (also repressive): "it may not even be a problem"
- **Sarcasm:** weaponised humour to deflect
- **Changing topic:** redirecting away from feeling territory
- **Arguing/intellectualising in the transference:** engaging head instead of heart
- **Gaze avoidance:** non-verbal tactic
- **Crossed arms/legs:** physical closure
- **Laughing off:** social defence
- **Compliance without will:** going through the motions while internally absent

### The Pillar Defence
The most frequently occurring defence in a given patient. Identified by analysing transcripts — whatever defence appears most across exchanges, that is the pillar. Focus treatment on this one first.

### Detecting Defence vs. Feeling
- **Words say one thing; body says another** → listen to the body
- If patient sighs while verbally denying feeling → feeling is present, defence is operating
- If patient says "I don't know" immediately without looking within → denial, not genuine uncertainty
- "I don't have a feeling" said instantly = deadening defence, not truth

## 4. Somatic Signatures of Core Feelings (Abbass & Town + Frederickson)

These are the specific physical pathways Davanloo identified through videotape research.

### Rage / Anger
- Upward movement of heat or energy from base of body → chest → neck → arms → hands
- Urge to grab, strike, clench
- Fists tighten, arms want to rise
- **Access question:** "Where do you feel that anger physically? Can you feel it in your body? What do your hands want to do?"

### Guilt About Rage
- Waves of upper body constriction
- Physical remorse — like being squeezed in the chest
- Mental image of the person harmed (often dead or collapsed)
- **Follow-up:** "When you see what you've done to him in your mind, what do you feel?"

### Grief / Sadness
- Softer than guilt — no sharp physical pain
- Heaviness in the chest
- Tears rising, fullness behind the eyes
- Wave of sadness moving through the face
- Cognitive content = loss, finality, "flood of losses"
- **Distinguish from anxiety:** tension is anxiety; heaviness/tearfulness is grief

### Fear
- Often shows as immobilisation or hypervigilance
- Tightening in the throat or chest without the upward heat of anger
- The "pit" or "hollow" sensation — absence of floor in the chest

### Love / Closeness
- Warmth in the chest
- Softening of the body — shoulders drop
- Impulse to reach toward, hold, protect

## 5. Central Dynamic Sequence

The phase structure of an ISTDP session (Frederickson + Abbass & Town):

### Phase 1: Inquiry + Psychodiagnosis
1. **Declare an internal problem** — not external circumstances, not someone else's problem
2. **Declare will to work on it** — if patient is complying with others' will, deactivate projection first
3. **Declare a specific example** — vagueness at this stage = tactical defence
4. Simultaneously: assess anxiety pathway (striated/smooth/CPD) to determine treatment format

### Phase 2: Pressure
- Consistent, gentle, persistent invitation to identify and experience underlying emotions
- Apply only when striated muscle pathway confirmed
- Goal: mobilise complex transference feelings (appreciation + irritation at therapist)

### Phase 3–4: Clarification + Challenge
- **Clarification:** "I notice you're slowing down and pulling back — do you see that?"
- **Challenge:** "So if you don't detach / don't intellectualise, what is the feeling here with me?"
- Target: visible signs of crystallising resistance (eye contact breaks, posture closing, pace slowing)

### Phase 5: Unlocking the Unconscious
- The "triggering mechanism" = patient has somatic experience of complex transference feelings
- When this happens: unconscious anxiety drops abruptly → resistance lifts → visual images + memories emerge
- This is the unconscious therapeutic alliance becoming dominant over unconscious resistance
- Therapist continues pressure until somatic experience occurs — not intellectual acknowledgement

### Phase 6 + 8: Recapitulation + Consolidation
- **Never interpret before breakthrough** — interpretation before feeling = reinforces intellectualisation
- After feeling: link feeling → anxiety → defence → past figures → present behaviour
- "So we can see how your rage toward your father got turned against yourself, and that became the depression..."
- Repetition of this linking across multiple domains cements the alliance and weakens resistance

## 6. Ego-Superego Separation Protocol (Frederickson)

This is the technical basis for the Punitive Critic work.

### The Core Distinction
- **Ego** = the patient's authentic healthy self — the part that wants to get well, wants connection, wants to be honest
- **Superego** = the attacking, punishing, self-sabotaging force — installed via early harsh treatment — operates through: anxiety, self-attack, self-dismissal, rushing past feelings, ignoring anxiety

### How to Identify Which Is Speaking
- **Ego:** "I want to get this out of me." "I know this isn't right." "I want to understand." Statements of desire, longing, will.
- **Superego:** "I'm stupid." "I can't do this." "It's too scary." "I don't deserve." The critic attacking the self.
- **Critical distinction:** the superego often disguises itself as realistic assessment — "I'm just facing the truth" — when it's actually attacking

### Separation Technique — Step by Step

**Step 1 — Identify the ego's healthy desire:**
"You said you want to get help today. That's your will. That's you — not me, not anyone else — saying what you want."

**Step 2 — Identify the superego as a separate attacking force:**
"And yet the moment you say that, this anxiety comes in and strangles you by the gut. Isn't it awful? You want to look at yourself and this force — not you — comes in and attacks you at the very moment you try."

**Step 3 — Causality clarification:**
"It's not me making this unpleasant. It's this force — this anxiety — that's making it unpleasant. I don't want that for you."

**Step 4 — Invite the ego to stand up to the superego:**
"So what does your healthy part say about this force that attacks you every time you try to help yourself?"

**Step 5 — Deactivate projection of will:**
If patient says "You want me to talk about this" → "Is it your will to be here? What inside you tells you that?" → patient must own desire internally

**Step 6 — Draw attention to superego pathology as it operates:**
- Ignoring anxiety: "When you ignore it, it builds. The anxiety isn't going anywhere — it's just below your awareness."
- Talking over anxiety: "Just now you kept talking even as the anxiety rose. What do you notice when you do that?"
- Self-attack during emotion: "As soon as you started to feel, a voice called you foolish. Do you see that? That's not you. That's the Critic attacking you at the moment of your greatest honesty."

### Integration Into Breakthrough Programme
The Punitive Critic work in the programme IS the superego. The separation technique is:
1. **Name it as separate** from the patient's true self
2. **Show its function** (keeping patient away from love/feeling)
3. **Show its origin** (installed by early harsh treatment — it's not the truth, it's an old voice)
4. **Invite the ego to respond** to the superego rather than accept its attacks

## 7. Self-Supervision Framework (Frederickson)

### The Checklist — Analyse Every Session Against This

1. **Did the patient declare an internal problem?** If not: address defences against declaring
2. **Did the patient declare will to work on it?** If not: deactivate projection of will, clarify ambivalence
3. **Did the patient give a specific example?** If not: address vagueness
4. **Analyse each response:** feeling / anxiety / defence?
5. **Is the patient avoiding feeling, or avoiding contact with me?** Different interventions required

### Intervention Categorisation
Categorise every intervention as:
- **Exploring feeling** — the target category
- **Regulating anxiety** — then return to feeling
- **Addressing defence + returning to feeling** — the one-two punch
- **Cognising** — reduce this to near zero

**The one-two punch rule:** Never address a defence without immediately inviting the patient back to feeling. Address defence THEN ask: "So if you don't [defend], what is the feeling?"

### The Critical Distinction
Two reasons therapy stalls:
1. **Patient avoiding feeling** → address defences against feeling
2. **Patient avoiding contact with therapist** → shift to transference → "What is the feeling toward me that makes you put up this wall?"
*If nothing is shifting after correct defence work, check for transference resistance.*

### Timing Rule
Intervene after the **first** defence — not the fourth. Every defence you allow to pass:
- Pushes the patient further from their feelings
- Increases suffering and regression in the session
Speed up tempo.

### Reading the Unconscious Signal
- **Sigh** = unconscious anxiety = feeling is mobilised = intervention is working
- **No sigh** = feeling not mobilised = assess why (10 reasons in Frederickson's list)
- Most important of the 10: smooth/CPD discharge (patient looks calm but feeling is draining elsewhere)
- With video: turn off sound → watch only for sighs → identify what triggered each sigh → those were the effective interventions

## 8. Psychodiagnostic Decision Tree

### Step 1: Assess Anxiety Pathway
Apply gentle pressure (invite feeling) and observe body response.

```
Patient sighs, tenses, hands clench → STRIATED
         ↓
Direct mobilisation — proceed with pressure and challenge

Patient looks calm but reports nausea/headache/GI → SMOOTH MUSCLE
         ↓
Stop pressure — regulate anxiety — capacity building — slow approach

Patient reports blurry vision / confusion / dissociation → CPD
         ↓
Stop immediately — ground — support — do NOT pursue feeling

Patient goes limp / muscle weakness → MOTOR CONVERSION
         ↓
Treat like smooth muscle — regulate first
```

### Step 2: Assess Resistance Level
From initial pressure response, determine:
- **Low/Moderate resistant:** striated anxiety, isolation of affect, engages when defences addressed → direct ISTDP format
- **High resistant:** characterological defences, transference resistance, needs sustained clarification/challenge before unlocking
- **Fragile:** smooth/CPD pathway, projection/splitting, history of trauma/dissociation → graded format, slower, compassion-first

### Step 3: Treatment Format Decision
| Pathway | Resistance | Format |
|---|---|---|
| Striated | Low-moderate | Standard ISTDP pressure |
| Striated | High | Sustained challenge before pressure |
| Smooth/CPD | Any | Graded format, anxiety regulation first |
| Mixed | Any | Start graded, shift format as capacity builds |

## 9. Rage-Guilt-Grief Sequence — Somatic Detail

This is Davanloo's foundational discovery: when the unconscious is unlocked, the sequence follows a specific order.

### The Sequence

**1. RAGE** (often comes first with mixed/complex feelings)
- Somatic: heat rising upward, arms activating, hands wanting to grab or strike
- Access: "Where in your body? What do your hands want to do? If you don't protect me from it — what does it want to do?"
- May include a violent impulse image (this is normal and necessary, not dangerous)

**2. GUILT** (emerges after rage is felt)
- Somatic: upper body constriction, waves of remorse
- Mental image: person harmed, often looking dead or collapsed
- Access: "What happens when you see what your rage did to him? What do you feel when you see his face?"

**3. GRIEF** (follows guilt — the deepest layer)
- Somatic: softer, heaviness in chest, tears, wave of sadness
- Cognitive: loss, regret, "flood of losses," what could have been
- Access: "Let it come. All of it. You've been carrying this for so long."

### Why This Sequence Matters
- Pressure → rage surfaces → if rage is fully felt → guilt emerges naturally
- Guilt → remorse for harm done → if followed → grief arises
- Grief = deepest healing layer — the mourning of what was lost, what was done, what can never be undone
- Therapy often stalls when rage is addressed but guilt and grief are not followed through

### When the Sequence Stalls
- **Rage stalls:** defence of turning anger on self (self-attack replaces outward anger) — separate ego/superego, redirect outward
- **Guilt stalls:** intellectualisation after breakthrough — return to image, return to feeling
- **Grief stalls:** self-attack begins ("I'm so pathetic for crying") — block immediately: "Not a time for judgment. A time for grief."

## 10. Exact Intervention Language Library

### Defence Labelling
- Intellectualisation: *"That's a thought, not a feeling. If you don't cover it with that thought, what is the feeling?"*
- Vagueness: *"That's vague — it tells us what you don't feel but not what you do feel. What is the feeling?"*
- Denial: *"You said 'not real happy' — something tells you that. What is that something?"*
- Detachment: *"I notice you're pulling back right now. What's the feeling that's making you pull back?"*
- Self-attack during feeling: *"If you don't judge yourself right now. Don't judge yourself. There's so much pain here."*
- Deadening: *"The only time we have no feelings is when we're six feet under. You're not dead yet."*
- Projection: *"You said I want to ask you things — but is it YOUR will to be here? What tells you inside that this is what you want?"*
- Helplessness: *"I'm not so sure about that. Because just a moment ago you..."*

### Returning to Feeling After Defence
- *"So if you don't [defend], what is the feeling toward [person]?"*
- *"Set that aside for a moment. What's happening in your body right now?"*
- *"Your body is saying something different from your words. What is it?"*
- *"Let's come back to the feeling. Where in your body?"*

### Pressure Interventions
- *"What is the feeling here with me right now?"*
- *"Besides anxious — what feeling is underneath that?"*
- *"If you don't protect me from it — what does it want to do?"*
- *"Let's see how you physically experience that in your body."*

### Regulating Anxiety
- *"Just pause. Take a breath. What do you notice in your body right now?"*
- *"We're going to go as slowly as you need. No right to go faster than you can."*
- *"Let's just pay close attention to this anxiety — where is it exactly?"*

### Ego-Superego Separation Language
- *"Isn't it awful? You want to help yourself and this force comes in and strangles you by the gut at the very moment you try."*
- *"That voice — that's not you. That's the critic your system installed. Your healthy part knows this isn't right."*
- *"The Critic is doing to you exactly what was done to you. But that's not the truth. That's just an old voice."*
- *"Is it your will to be here? Not what anyone told you — what do YOU want?"*

### After Breakthrough to Feeling
- *"Stay right there. Don't move away."*
- *"Let it come. All of it."*
- *"Don't hold your breath. There's more where that came from."*
- *"Just stay here with me."*
- *"Let it out. You've been carrying this for so long."*

## 11. Key Clinical Distinctions

### Feeling vs. Anxiety vs. Defence — Quick Reference
| What patient says/does | Classification |
|---|---|
| "I feel sad" + chest heaviness + tears | Feeling |
| "I feel anxious" / sighs / tense | Anxiety (striated) |
| Nausea, reports stomach upset | Anxiety (smooth) |
| "I don't know" / vague | Defence (vagueness/denial) |
| "I think he was wrong" (about feeling question) | Defence (intellectualisation) |
| Changes topic | Defence (tactical) |
| "I just feel detached" | Defence (detachment) |
| Self-attacks when vulnerable | Defence (character/superego) |
| Cries but holds breath to stop | Defence against feeling (freeze) |

### When NOT to Apply Pressure
- Smooth muscle or CPD anxiety pathway active
- Patient is dissociating
- Patient has history of fragility (check Frederickson's fragile patient indicators)
- Patient is in active self-harm
- Alliance is rupturing (clarify, repair, then return)

### Transference Resistance — When It's the Primary Target
Signs:
- Correct defence work is happening but no sighs, no movement
- Patient is engaging head but not body
- Session feels like debate rather than experience
Intervention: shift from "what's the feeling about X" to "what's the feeling toward me that's creating this wall between us?"

## 12. Unconscious Therapeutic Alliance

The concept underpinning the entire ISTDP approach (Davanloo via Abbass & Town):

- Two forces operate simultaneously in every patient: **unconscious therapeutic alliance** (UTA) and **unconscious resistance**
- UTA = the part of the patient that wants to heal, wants connection, is oriented toward health
- Resistance = the system of defences built to survive early trauma
- Goal of treatment: help UTA dominate over resistance
- Indicator of UTA rising: sighing, eye contact, spontaneous disclosure, somatic opening, imagery, tears
- Indicator of resistance rising: eye contact breaking, posture closing, pace slowing, tactical defences increasing
- After breakthrough: UTA takes over → patient accesses visual images, clear memories, spontaneous connections
- Consolidation strengthens UTA; accepting defences weakens it

## 13. Resistance Spectrum — Full Classification (Kuhn/Abbass)

Patients exist on a spectrum from very low to very high resistance. Psychodiagnosis means locating where they are.

### The Five-Level Spectrum (Davanloo)
Simplified into three working categories (Abbass):
- **Low resistance:** breakthroughs occur with inquiry and pressure alone; grief and tactical defences only
- **Moderate resistance:** requires pressure + clarification; feelings break through at mid-rise
- **High resistance:** requires sustained pressure + clarification + challenge (head-on collision); resistance is crystallised in the transference
- **High resistance with repression:** goes flat (smooth/CPD) under pressure — graded format needed
- **Fragile:** goes flat very early, primitive defences (projection, splitting, dissociation) — graded format with structural integration first

### The Psychodiagnosis Algorithm (Abbass 2007)
```
Start: Inquiry + Pressure
        ↓
Patient signals (striated anxiety) and uses tactical defences
        ↓
Continue pressure
        ↓
Feelings break through?
  YES → Low resistance
  NO → Resistance going into the transference (mid-rise)
        ↓
Add Clarification + mild Challenge
        ↓
Feelings break through?
  YES → Moderate resistance
  NO → High rise: resistance crystallised IN the transference
        ↓
Add systematic Challenge including Head-On Collision
        ↓
Feelings break through?
  YES → High resistance
  NO → Patient goes FLAT at any point
        ↓
High resistance with repression OR Fragile → Graded Format
```

### Rise in the Transference — Calibration Guide
| Rise Level | Signs | Interventions |
|---|---|---|
| Low rise | Minimal anxiety, few defences | Inquiry, pressure only — avoid clarification/challenge |
| Mid-rise | Some signalling, tactical defences, some eye-contact breaks | Pressure + clarification + mild challenge |
| High rise | Heavy crystallisation, high tension, patient battling own defences ("intrapsychic crisis") | Pressure + challenge + head-on collision |

## 14. Functions of Defence/Resistance Hierarchy (Kuhn)

As resistance increases, defences stop operating in isolation and form integrated systems. This determines the *level* of intervention required.

| Defence Function | Name | What It Looks Like |
|---|---|---|
| Avoid feeling | REF (Resistance to Experiencing Feeling) | Intellectualisation, vagueness, denial — the "cellar door" |
| Avoid closeness | REC (Resistance to Emotional Closeness) | Withdrawal, detachment, distancing from therapist — the "front door" |
| Enact pathological relationship | Character/Transference Resistance | Compliance, defiance, passive helplessness, dependency |
| Punish/sabotage self | Superego Resistance (PSE) | Self-attack, self-sabotage, unconscious guilt driving symptoms |

**Clinical implication:** If addressing REF (defences against feeling) yields nothing, the patient may be operating at REC (avoiding closeness). Shift focus to the relationship and ask about feelings toward the therapist.

## 15. Syntonicity — The Hidden Obstacle (Kuhn)

Syntonic defences are experienced by the patient as *who they are* — not as defences.

**Signs of syntonicity:**
- Patient says "I'm just like that" / "that's just how I am"
- Challenge is experienced as personal attack, not help
- Challenge produces anger without accompanying appreciation (no CTF rise)
- CTF falls rather than rises when challenged
- No sighing — no unconscious signalling

**What to do:**
1. Do NOT challenge syntonic defences directly — it fails and creates misalliance
2. Clarify extensively first: "Do you notice you are doing this? Does this help you or hurt you?"
3. The goal is the patient beginning to *separate themselves from the defence* — "incipient dystonia"
4. Sighing will begin as the patient starts to separate from the defence (UTA activating)

**Key question sequence for defence-to-dystonia:**
1. *"Do you see that you are [doing this]?"*
2. *"Is this helping you or hurting you?"* — clarifying costs is the key conscious step
3. *"What feelings come up when you see you're hurting yourself this way?"* — experiencing sadness over losses due to the defence is the key unconscious step
4. *"Would you like to see what we can do about this together?"*

## 16. Triangle of Person (Malan/Davanloo via Kuhn)

The triangle of conflict plays out across three relationship domains simultaneously.

```
         Therapist (T)
              /\
             /  \
            /    \
      Past (P) — Current (C)
```

**The three domains:**
- **T (Therapist/Transference):** What feelings, anxiety, and defences are operating right now in the room with the therapist
- **C (Current):** How the same pattern shows up in present-day relationships (partner, children, boss)
- **P (Past):** The origin — the early attachment figures where the pattern was learned

**Clinical application:**
- The same feeling/anxiety/defence triangle operates identically in all three corners
- Linking T–C–P is what makes change generalise beyond the session
- Transference work (T) is the most powerful entry point because it is happening live
- After breakthrough: "We can see how this same feeling — which you just felt toward me — is the same feeling that runs your relationship with your father... and your wife"

## 17. Intrapsychic Crisis — The Tipping Point (Davanloo via Kuhn)

The intrapsychic crisis is the moment just before breakthrough — and the most critical intervention point.

**What it is:** "An extremely complex state within the patient, one in which he both wishes to cling to his resistance ever more strongly and at the same time begins to turn against it." — Davanloo

**Signs:**
- Patient is visibly battling — wants to defend and wants to open simultaneously
- Very high tension, crystallised resistance, but also strong UTA signals
- Sighing increasing even as defences intensify
- Patient may say "I don't know why I keep doing this" — ego beginning to separate from defence

**What to do:**
- This is the moment for head-on collision and sustained challenge
- Do NOT back off — this is exactly when maintaining pressure tips the balance toward UTA
- Do NOT interpret — that will abort the crisis by shifting it to intellectual territory
- Goal: help UTA dominate resistance → unlocking follows

## 18. Dysregulated Affective States (DAS) — The Key Clinical Framework (Frederickson, Messina & Grecucci 2018)

This is the most clinically precise framework for understanding when and how to intervene. Critically important for deciding whether to PRESS TOWARD feeling or REGULATE FIRST.

### The Core Distinction
Not all emotional distress is the same. Two fundamentally different types:

**Type 1 — Emotion + Dysregulated Anxiety (DAS-Anxiety)**
- The original feeling is adaptive and real
- But it is accompanied by overwhelming anxiety (parasympathetic NS activation)
- The patient is dysregulated by the *anxiety*, not the *emotion*
- **Treatment:** Regulate the anxiety first → then the original feeling can be experienced
- Do NOT press toward feeling while parasympathetic anxiety is high — this worsens dysregulation

**Type 2 — Defensive Affects (DAS-Defence)**
- The patient is experiencing a *secondary/defensive affect* created by a defence mechanism, not the original emotion
- E.g., projection → fear of others' imaginary anger (not the patient's own anger)
- The defensive affect is proportional to the defence, not the stimulus — it stays high as long as the defence operates
- **Treatment:** Block the defence → the defensive affect disappears → then access the original emotion underneath

### Original Emotions vs Defensive Affects — Critical Table

| Type | What to do | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Original adaptive feelings** | **EXPRESS** — experience fully | Proportional anger, good grief/crying, healthy guilt, genuine love, real fear |
| **Defensive affects** | **BLOCK/REGULATE** | Weepiness (covering anger), protest crying, anger from projection, neurotic guilt, shame covering guilt |

**Key clinical examples:**
- **Good crying** (grief over real loss) → express, deepen, stay with it
- **Weepiness** (tears as a defence against anger or as helplessness) → block: *"Notice the tears are coming — but what's underneath? What's the feeling before the tears?"*
- **Healthy guilt** (genuine remorse for harm done) → express
- **Neurotic guilt** (punishing self in excess, or guilt without the underlying rage) → block, look for rage underneath
- **Proportional anger** (angry at stimulus for 10 seconds) → express
- **Anger from projection** (furious at imaginary threat, proportional to hours of projection) → block projection first, then find original feeling

### The Wave Shape Distinction
- **Original emotions:** wave shape — rise after stimulus, fall when stimulus ends or adaptive action taken
- **Defensive affects:** flat-top shape — rise rapidly with defence, stay high as long as defence operates, do not naturally resolve

### Sympathetic vs Parasympathetic Anxiety — Detailed Table

| Pathway | Symptoms | Dysregulation Level | Action |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Somatic NS (striated)** | Hand clenching, tension in chest/intercostals (sighing), arm/shoulder/neck/leg tension, jaw clenching | None / mild | Continue — watch anxiety while encouraging expression |
| **Parasympathetic NS** | Bladder urgency, GI spasm, IBS, nausea, vomiting, migraines, hypertension, asthma, "jelly legs", drifting, dissociation | Moderate–severe | **Regulate anxiety first** |
| **Cognitive-Perceptual System** | Hallucinations, severe dissociation, thought blocking, tunnel vision, tinnitus | Severe | **Regulate immediately** — do not press toward feeling |

### Four-Phase EDER Methodology
1. **Emotion Elicitation** — specific example → invite feeling → address defences until feeling emerges
2. **Regulatory Mechanism Enhancement** — build awareness of what's happening; differentiate feeling from anxiety from defensive affect
3. **Dysregulatory Mechanism Reduction** — regulate anxiety OR block defensive affect (whichever is operating)
4. **Full Emotional Experience** — once DAS resolved: label feeling + experience physically in body + experience impulse + express

## 19. Graded Format — For Fragile and High-Resistance-with-Repression Patients (Frederickson)

When a patient goes flat (smooth/CPD anxiety, dissociation, motor conversion), standard ISTDP pressure is contraindicated. The Graded technique builds three capacities first.

### The Three Capacities to Build
1. **Attention capacity** — can the patient maintain present-moment awareness? If not, keep redirecting: *"Stay here with me. What do you notice right now in your body?"*
2. **Anxiety regulation capacity** — can the patient observe anxiety without being overwhelmed? *"What do you notice in your body? Let's just pay attention to this together."*
3. **Affect experience capacity** — can the patient tolerate even a small rise in feeling without going flat? Work in very small doses.

### Signs That a Patient Needs Graded Format
- Eyes glaze over, legs tremble, body freezes, words become disjointed on inquiry
- Primary dissociation: unable to stay in present moment, memory fragmentation
- Secondary dissociation: emotional numbing, depersonalisation
- Smooth muscle or CPD anxiety pathway active
- History of trauma with incomplete integration

### Graded Format Approach
- Do NOT pursue feeling until attention and anxiety regulation are established
- Work only with what is in front: *"I notice you just went somewhere else. Come back here. What do you notice right now?"*
- Very small doses of emotional material — titrate carefully
- After each small opening: return to grounding
- Goal of graded work: shift anxiety pathway from smooth/CPD to striated — then standard ISTDP becomes possible

### The Somatic Case (Frederickson) — Key Finding
A patient with 13 years of insomnia, panic attacks, bowel complaints, headaches, and physical collapses (unable to work, conducting business from bed) — all symptoms perceived as biological, no psychological insight — achieved "nearly complete remission" following two breakthroughs of the sadistic impulse, guilt, and grief. This is direct evidence: somatic symptoms often ARE repressed emotion. Once the emotion is experienced, the body stops needing to express it through symptoms.

## 20. Transference Resistance — Specific Types (Frederickson via Kuhn)

Transference resistance is an "invitation to a sick relationship." Recognising the specific type prevents the therapist from unconsciously accepting the invitation.

| Type | What patient does | Therapist trap |
|---|---|---|
| Omnipotent therapist | Patient passively waits for therapist to fix them | Therapist accepts expert/authority role |
| Helpless patient | Constant "I can't / I don't know" | Therapist does all the work |
| Dependent patient | Clinging, constant reassurance-seeking | Therapist provides reassurance rather than addressing defence |
| Passive-detached | Goes through motions with no genuine engagement | Therapist keeps talking into the silence |
| Defiant patient | Argues with, dismisses, or challenges every intervention | Therapist debates or backs down |
| Highly self-critical | Self-attack in response to every intervention | Therapist focuses on defences instead of protecting the patient from self-attack |

**"Be counterprojective" (Davanloo via Kuhn):** Therapist must "step out of the shoes of the parent." Don't accept the role being projected onto you. Use language like:
- *"You're the expert on you."*
- *"What do you think?"*
- *"Shall we look at this together, if you'd like to?"* (mini-consents to confirm will, especially with defiant patients)

**Transference resistance is "the silent killer of therapy"** — patients passively waiting for an omnipotent therapist can maintain good eye contact and no sighing indefinitely, giving no visible signal that therapy is failing. If there is no signalling after correct defence work, ask directly: *"I wonder if part of you is waiting passively for me to solve this for you?"* — sighing will confirm or disconfirm.

## 21. Five Monitoring Factors — Per Session (Abbass via Kuhn)

After every significant exchange, check:

1. **Anxiety pathway:** Striated? Smooth muscle? CPD? Mixed?
2. **Anxiety threshold:** How quickly does anxiety exceed the patient's tolerance? Is the threshold rising (progress) or falling (regression)?
3. **Active major defence pattern:** Isolation of affect (striated)? Repression/self-attack (smooth)? Projection/splitting (CPD)?
4. **Syntonicity vs dystonicity:** Is the patient identified with the defence, or beginning to see it as alien?
5. **Psychodiagnostic category:** Low / moderate / high resistance / high with repression / fragile?

## 22. ISTDP Evidence Base — Effect Sizes and Outcomes

### Meta-Analytic Summary (Abbass, Town, Driessen 2012 + Lilliengren 2016)

**Effect sizes from 28+ RCTs:**
- General symptom improvement: **d = 0.97** at treatment end → **d = 1.51** at long-term follow-up (>9 months)
- Somatic symptoms: d = 0.81 → **d = 2.21** at follow-up
- Anxiety: d = 1.08 → d = 1.35 at follow-up
- Depression: d = 0.59 → d = 0.98 at follow-up
- Interpersonal problems: d = 0.84 at treatment end
- Outperforms other bona fide treatments at follow-up

**Conditions with RCT support:** Depression (including treatment-resistant), anxiety disorders, panic, somatoform/functional somatic disorders, eating disorders, substance-related disorders, personality disorders, borderline PD, chronic pain.

**Treatment-resistant depression (Town et al., 2017 RCT, 16 sessions):**
- 36% full remission with ISTDP vs 3.7% with treatment as usual

**Personality disorders:**
- Psychodynamic therapy ES = 1.46 pre→post (37-week mean, 1.5yr follow-up)
- Short-term psychodynamic for PD: ES = 0.91 general symptoms, 0.97 interpersonal (Messer & Abbass)
- Psychodynamic therapy for BPD: 87% TAU still met criteria at 5yr follow-up vs 13% psychodynamic (Bateman & Fonagy, 2008)

### Key Finding: Benefits Increase After Treatment Ends
- Unlike CBT/medication whose effects decay — psychodynamic/ISTDP effects **continue to grow** post-treatment
- Confirmed in at least 5 independent meta-analyses
- Likely mechanism: treatment sets in motion psychological processes that continue operating

### Comparison to Medication
- Antidepressant effect sizes (FDA data): fluoxetine d=0.26, sertraline d=0.26, average d=0.31
- ISTDP effect sizes 3–5x larger than antidepressants for the same conditions
- Psychodynamic therapy ES = 0.97 (short-term) vs antidepressants ES = 0.31

### Shedler's "Active Ingredients" Finding
Research using the Psychotherapy Process Q-Sort (archival session transcripts across both CBT and psychodynamic treatments):
- **Adherence to psychodynamic process prototype predicted good outcome in BOTH psychodynamic AND cognitive therapy**
- Adherence to the CBT process prototype showed little/no relation to outcome in either treatment
- Implication: the effective ingredients of therapy across modalities are psychodynamic in nature
- Key psychodynamic process markers: focus on affect/emotion, exploration of avoidance/defence, recurring themes/patterns, past-present links, therapy relationship as data, patient's inner experience (not just cognition)

### Unlocking vs No-Unlocking Outcome Comparison (Abbass, Town et al., N=500)
- **Symptom improvement:** Unlocking group ES = 0.91 vs No-unlocking ES = 0.28
- **Interpersonal improvement:** Unlocking group ES = 0.37 vs No-unlocking ES = 0.12
- Clinical implication: whether the unconscious is unlocked in the trial therapy predicts the entire course of treatment outcome

## 23. Cost-Effectiveness Data (Abbass)

### Hospital + Doctor Cost Offsets
**N=890 patients, 57 therapists, mean 7.3 sessions (Abbass, Kisely, Rasic, Town, Johansson 2015):**
- 1yr pre: ~$12,000/patient
- 1yr post: ~$2,500/patient
- 2yr post: ~$2,000/patient
- 3yr post: ~$1,800/patient (near normal population costs)
- **17x cost offset** — healthcare costs drop to population normal levels within 3 years

**Trial therapy cost effects (N=344, Abbass, Kisely, Town 2018):**
- Significant cost reductions from year 1 post through year 3 post vs 1yr pre
- Costs approach normal population levels within 2 years

**Implication for Breakthrough context:** Intensive brief treatment (7–20 sessions) may be more cost-effective than years of symptom management. Somatic symptoms (Sapandeep's chronic pain, GI, etc.) are primary cost drivers — ISTDP addresses these directly.

## 24. Psychodiagnosis Algorithm — Complete (Abbass)

The psychodiagnosis determines format: standard ISTDP vs graded/capacity-building.

### Step 1: Encourage presence with all feelings
Gentle opening invitation. Monitor what happens.

### Step 2: Apply pressure (encouraging healthy action — "what do you notice?", "can we look at this together?")
Watch the anxiety and defence response.

### Step 3: Identify which of 6 response patterns occurs:

| Response | What you see | Diagnosis | Treatment |
|---|---|---|---|
| Feels feelings immediately | Warmth, softening, imagery | **Low resistance** | Complete in 1–2 sessions |
| Tense then feel with pressure | Striated muscle anxiety then emotional access | **Moderate resistance** | Standard ISTDP pressure → challenge → HOC; up to 10 sessions |
| Tense and defend | Striated muscle anxiety + isolation of affect | **High resistance** | Pressure → challenge → head-on collision → unlocking |
| Go flat: depression/fatigue | Smooth muscle, motor conversion, depression | **High resistance + repression** | Repeated unlocking phases; longer treatment |
| Go flat: confusion/fear | CPD, projection, splitting, primitive defences | **Fragile character structure** | Capacity building format first |
| No response | Nothing visible | Search for hidden anxiety | Probe more specifically |

### 4 Major Defence Types (mapped to anxiety pathway)
1. **Isolation of affect** → striated muscle anxiety
2. **Repression** → smooth muscle anxiety / motor conversion
3. **Primitive defences** (projection, splitting, projective identification) → CPD
4. **Resistance of guilt** (punitive superego) → self-defeating, self-sabotaging patterns across all levels

## 25. Somatic Pathways of Each Feeling (Abbass — Complete List)

| Feeling | Somatic pathway |
|---|---|
| **Love** | Rising warmth in chest; urge to smile and embrace |
| **Rage** | Rising heat/energy up chest to head, then down arms; tension, anxiety, and somatic symptoms drop or stop |
| **Guilt about rage** | Hard, solid waves; pain in upper chest; felt as if one has just murdered a loved one |
| **Grief** | Tears; painful feeling in chest; waves less distinct and hard than guilt; not as loud |

**Clinical implication:** These somatic markers are the evidence that feeling has been ACTUALLY accessed (not intellectualised). If the patient reports rage but shows none of the heat/energy pathway, the feeling is being described, not experienced. The therapist must keep pressure until the somatic pathway activates.

**Rage pathway neurobiologically:** Rage travels up the same voluntary muscle system that carries anxiety — as rage rises, anxiety goes DOWN the body and resolves somatic symptoms. This is why somatic symptoms resolve after emotional breakthrough: the same pathway cannot carry both simultaneously.

## 26. Functional Somatic Disorders — ISTDP Protocol (Abbass 2024)

### Mechanism
Bond with parents + trauma + pain + fear → rage and guilt about rage → somatic symptoms / character disorder / other symptoms (and intergenerational transmission).

The somatic symptom IS the emotion, expressed through the body instead of consciously. When emotion is fully experienced, the body stops needing to carry it.

### FSD Evidence Base
- 50+ published ISTDP outcome studies for FSD
- Conditions treated: urethral syndrome / pelvic pain, back pain, functional movement disorders, chronic headache, pseudoseizures, chronic pain, IBS, mixed MUS, atopic dermatitis, bruxism, FND
- Effects sustained or increase at follow-up (Town & Driessen 2013)
- ISTDP outperforms CBT for chronic pain (Abbass et al., 2022)
- Emotional focus is the key predictive variable for somatic outcomes (Town et al., 2019)
- 77.8% of somatic disorder studies reported reductions in healthcare utilisation

### Somatic Symptom Mapping to Anxiety Pathway
| Pathway | Symptoms |
|---|---|
| **Striated muscle** | Fibromyalgia, MSK pain, headache, chest pain, shortness of breath, hyperventilation |
| **Smooth muscle** | GI conditions (IBS, spasm), migraine, coronary artery spasm, bronchospasm, bladder symptoms |
| **Cognitive-perceptual disruption** | Functional neurological disorders, pseudoseizures, functional movement disorders, visual disturbances |
| **Motor conversion** | Weakness/paralysis without organic cause; person appears relaxed; no striated muscle tension |

### Sympathy Symptoms (Projective Identification Into Body)
- Patient with repression carries "projective identification" related to rage in the unconscious
- This creates **sympathy symptoms** — body symptoms that mirror the physical experience of attacking or being attacked
- E.g., patient who unconsciously wants to strangle someone may have chronic throat pain or dysphagia
- E.g., patient with unconscious murderous rage toward a parent may develop the illness the parent had
- **Clinical recognition:** when somatic symptoms have a symbolic or interpersonal logic, consider projective identification

## 27. Bracing Technique (Abbass)

Bracing is used when the patient is oscillating around the threshold between striated muscle anxiety (manageable) and repression/CPD (too high).

### When to Use
- Patient rises to near-threshold anxiety but doesn't break through to feeling
- Patient gets tense then goes flat before feelings emerge
- In repression cases where the therapeutic window is narrow

### The Technique
Combine **pressure** (evoking feelings, activating somatic pathway of rage) with **recap** (self-reflection, linking phenomena, observing body and thoughts) simultaneously or in rapid alternation:

- **Pressure:** *"What do you notice in your body right now?"* / *"What do you feel toward [person] when you really let yourself notice?"*
- **Recap / anxiety reduction:** *"Just notice — your fist is clenching, your chest tightening. Let's look at that. What's that about?"* / *"We can see: you start to feel something, and immediately this [defence] kicks in..."*

### Why It Works (Neurobiological Basis)
- Pressure fires limbic areas (amygdala, emotional memory)
- Recap fires self-reflective brain areas (PFC, insula)
- Doing both simultaneously **trains the brain to fire both functional regions together** — building the neurological capacity for integrated emotional processing
- Below threshold: more pressure to evoke feelings
- Above threshold: more recap to reduce anxiety
- At threshold: bracing — both together

## 28. Portraying Technique (Abbass)

### What It Is
Portraying rage means **verbally or physically enacting** the impulse — describing what the rage "would do" if it could act — before the patient has capacity to fully experience the feeling internally.

### When to Use
- Patient cannot yet tolerate the full somatic experience of rage
- Patient has only partial contact with impulse (mid-rise in CTF, minimum)
- Building capacity in fragile or highly repressed patients

### How to Do It
1. Use 3rd or 4th parties to make it easier: *"If your rage could speak to your father right now through you, what would it say? What would it do?"*
2. Escalate toward physicality: *"And if there were no consequences whatsoever, what would the rage actually do?"*
3. Focus immediately on **guilt about the rage** — this is what builds capacity: *"And what happens in your body when you let yourself see that image? What do you feel toward him for what he did?"*

### Why Guilt Is the Key (Abbass's Core Insight)
**Guilt is the central therapeutic lever.** Identifying and feeling guilt is the key to:
- Capacity building (guilt tolerance → rage tolerance → full emotional range)
- Symptom removal (guilt about rage is what drives somatic symptoms and self-punishment)
- Opening the unconscious (the UTA rises sharply when guilt is experienced)
- Healing attachment trauma (the guilt is always about the loved person who also hurt you)

The pathway is: **feel the rage → feel the guilt about the rage → the grief emerges → healing**.
Shortcuts that bypass guilt do not work. The grief that resolves trauma is always preceded by guilt.

## 29. Full Treatment Phase Arc — Fragile Character Structure (Abbass)

For patients who go flat (CPD or primitive defences) on initial pressure — standard ISTDP format is contraindicated. Use this arc:

### Phase 1: Capacity Building
- **Goal:** Shift anxiety pathway from CPD/smooth muscle to striated muscle
- **Method:** Graded format — very small rises in CTF, always keeping below CPD threshold
- **Tools:** Bracing (combining pressure + recap), portraying, psychoeducation about the model
- **Duration:** A few sessions to 25+ sessions depending on level of fragility
- **Marker of completion:** Patient can tolerate striated muscle anxiety without going to CPD/projection/splitting

### Phase 2: Repeated Unlocking
- **Goal:** Each session achieves at least partial unlocking of unconscious
- **Method:** Pressure → rise in CTF → crystallization of resistance in transference → challenge → HOC → breakthrough
- **What breaks through:** First breakthroughs tend to be mostly grief (not yet rage/guilt); this is partial unlocking
- **Partial unlocking:** Rage and guilt partly experienced; anxiety and defences drop proportionally; UTA has small advantage; links to past emerge
- **Major unlocking:** Rage and guilt fully experienced; image transfer occurs (current person morphs to past person in patient's experience); UTA dominant; direct access to unconscious
- **Between sessions:** Symptoms continue to reduce; patient reports spontaneous insight and emotional experiences outside sessions

### Phase 3: Working Through
- **Goal:** Generalise breakthroughs across all significant relationships (Triangle of Person)
- **Method:** After each unlocking, systematically trace the same feeling-anxiety-defence pattern in current relationships, past relationships, and the therapy relationship
- **Key:** Do not interpret before breakthrough. After breakthrough: link feeling → anxiety → defence → past figure → present behaviour
- **Consolidation:** Repeated linking across multiple domains cements UTA dominance and weakens resistance structurally

### Phase 4: Termination
- **Goal:** Consolidate gains, transfer healing capacity to patient
- **Content:** Grief about ending; often revisits original attachment wounds; patient says goodbye to the damaged or lost version of themselves
- **Risk:** Therapist moving too quickly → patient re-activates old defences to delay ending
- **Marker of readiness:** Patient can tolerate grief of ending without collapsing into depression or acting out

## 30. UTA Spectrum — Reading Where the Alliance Is (Abbass)

The therapist must track which UTA state the patient is in at all times:

| UTA State | What you observe | Clinical meaning |
|---|---|---|
| **R >> UTA (low rise)** | Negation, slips of the tongue, vivid dreams | Resistance dominant; UTA just beginning to stir; continue pressure |
| **R > UTA (mid rise)** | "Whispers from the alliance" — concise, spontaneous dynamic understanding | Resistance still dominant but UTA active; continue clarify/challenge |
| **R < UTA (partial unlocking)** | Rage and grief with clear linkages to past | UTA winning; maintain — help patient stay with feeling |
| **R << UTA (major unlocking)** | Rage and guilt; image transfer (present person becomes past person) | Full dominance of UTA; memory + emotion function exceeds inhibitory function |

**Why this matters:** The therapist's every intervention should aim to push the balance from R>>UTA toward R<<UTA. If an intervention causes R to increase (patient tenses further, goes flat, deflects), the approach needs to change.
